Level 1: Trends in Statistical Design (2010–2026)

  • Objective: To quantify the adoption of Bayesian Adaptive Designs and Master Protocols in NSCLC.

  • Methodology:

    • Data Source: AACT (Aggregate Analysis of ClinicalTrials.gov).

    • Feature Engineering: Regex-based classification on brief_title and intervention_model.

    • QA/QC: Filtered for incomplete observations (years > 2026) and low-sample-size noise.

5. Trends of Bayesian Adaptive Designs and Master Protocols

# Data acquisition and feature engineering
query <- glue_sql("
  SELECT 
    s.nct_id, s.brief_title, s.phase, s.start_date, s.overall_status, 
    d.intervention_model, d.primary_purpose 
  FROM studies s
  LEFT JOIN designs d ON s.nct_id = d.nct_id
  WHERE s.nct_id IN ({nsclc_ids*}) 
  AND s.start_date >= '2010-01-01'
", .con = con)

nsclc_enriched <- dbGetQuery(con, query) %>%
  mutate(
    year = as.integer(format(as.Date(start_date), "%Y")),
    is_adaptive = str_detect(tolower(brief_title), "bayesian|adaptive|seamless|sequential") | 
                  str_detect(toupper(intervention_model), "SEQUENTIAL|FACTORIAL"),
    is_master = str_detect(tolower(brief_title), "basket|umbrella|platform|master")
  )

# Data preprocessing and filtering
# Exclude data post-2026 and filter out years with insufficient sample sizes
nsclc_filtered <- nsclc_enriched %>%
  filter(year >= 2010, year <= 2026) %>% # Truncate incomplete years
  group_by(year) %>%
  mutate(year_total = n()) %>%           # Calculate total annual sample size
  ungroup() %>%
  filter(year_total > 5)                 # Remove years with n <= 5 (denoising)

# Trend analysis visualization
trend_data <- nsclc_filtered %>%
  group_by(year) %>%
  summarise(
    adaptive_pct = mean(is_adaptive, na.rm = TRUE) * 100
  )

# Generate the innovation penetration rate bar chart
plot_innovation <- ggplot(trend_data, aes(x = as.factor(year), y = adaptive_pct)) +

    title = "Innovation Penetration Rate in NSCLC (2010-2026)",

Key Findings:

  • The analysis shows that Phase II clinical trials consistently accounted for the largest proportion of NSCLC studies between 2010 and 2026, indicating that early-stage efficacy evaluation remains a major focus of drug development.

  • The proportion of adaptive trial designs increased over time, suggesting a gradual shift from traditional fixed designs to more flexible clinical trial strategies. This trend reflects the growing use of innovative trial methodologies in NSCLC research.

  • A simple comparison also showed that adaptive trials had a lower completion rate than conventional trials. One possible explanation is that adaptive designs are more likely to stop early when interim results indicate limited benefit, rather than continuing to study completion.

  • Overall, this exploratory analysis demonstrates changing clinical trial practices in NSCLC and provides a foundation for future analyses of trial design and development efficiency.

Level 2: Clinical Efficacy & Development Efficiency Analysis

  • Objective: To evaluate the impact of trial design strategies (traditional vs. adaptive/master protocols) on development cycles and clinical endpoint selection, assessing their contribution to overall R&D efficiency.

  • Methodology:

    • Data Engineering: Batch extraction via Glue; standardization of design features and clinical metrics.

    • Feature Normalization: Conversion of unstructured text into quantitative metrics (e.g., duration_months).

    • Statistical Approach: Comparative analysis using boxplots and the Wilcoxon rank-sum test to evaluate the influence of trial design on study duration.

    FROM outcomes

  ")
  
  all_outcomes_list[[i]] <- dbGetQuery(con, query_outcomes)
  message(paste("Processed batch", i, "of", length(nct_chunks)))
}

Key Findings

  • Efficiency Metrics: Statistical analysis (p>0.05) reveals no significant difference in trial duration between adaptive designs (via keyword matching) and traditional methods, suggesting that design complexity does not inherently accelerate development speed. The shorter observed durations in “NA” categories are primarily attributed to small-scale or non-interventional studies.

  • Strategic Endpoint Hierarchy: A distinct hierarchy is evident in clinical development: early-phase trials emphasize safety and rapid signals (ORR/PFS), whereas Phase III trials shift toward Overall Survival (OS) as the definitive measure of utility. The prevalence of OS in Phase I/II trials suggests a cautious, long-term monitoring strategy for molecularly targeted sub-cohorts.
